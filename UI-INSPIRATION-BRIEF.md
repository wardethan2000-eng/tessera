# Heirloom UI Inspiration Brief

This document is a design-direction brief for generating inspirational images, mood boards, and conceptual interface explorations for **Heirloom**.

It is not a technical implementation plan. It is a creative source-of-truth for image models, designers, and anyone trying to visualize what this product should feel like before translating it into code.

---

## One-Sentence Product Summary

**Heirloom is a quiet, private family archive for preserving people, relationships, stories, photographs, voices, recipes, places, and artifacts across generations.**

It is not a genealogy utility, not a social network, and not a dashboard. It is a living memorial and family memory archive.

---

## What The App Is For

Families lose irreplaceable knowledge constantly:

- the way a grandmother laughed
- the story behind a recipe card
- the voice of an elder answering a question
- the context behind an old photograph
- the texture of ordinary family life that never makes it into formal records

Heirloom exists to preserve that material with dignity.

The app should feel like:

- entering a literary archive
- opening a family monograph
- handling a box of letters, recipes, photographs, and voice notes
- drifting through memory rather than querying a database

The app should not feel like:

- Facebook for families
- Ancestry.com
- Google Photos
- Notion
- Airtable
- a content feed
- a productivity workspace
- an admin dashboard

---

## Core Emotional Target

The emotional center of the product is **quiet reverence**.

The interface should feel:

- warm
- restrained
- editorial
- archival
- atmospheric
- intimate
- slow in a good way
- carefully typeset
- emotionally safe

The interface should not feel:

- loud
- hyper-modern
- tech-demo shiny
- gamified
- productivity-first
- app-store generic
- neon
- corporate
- social
- juvenile

If a design looks like it is trying to maximize engagement, it is wrong.

If it feels like a beautifully designed room in which memory can rest, it is closer.

---

## The Product Metaphor

The app uses a spatial and emotional metaphor rather than a utility metaphor.

Major mental models:

- **Atrium**: the soft entry point into a family archive
- **Constellation**: the family tree as a living field of connected people
- **Drift**: slow, serendipitous movement through memories
- **Node / chapter**: each person has a page that reads like a chapter in a book

Users should feel like they are:

- entering a family archive
- beginning from one memory
- moving outward into people and relationships
- discovering connections
- spending time with presence, not just information

---

## Visual World

The visual world should sit at the intersection of:

- archival museum
- literary monograph
- letterpress book
- still garden
- private reading room
- family bible
- curated memory box

Useful real-world reference energy:

- Kinfolk, but less lifestyle-coded
- the British Library online collections
- old family albums presented with editorial restraint
- quiet museum exhibit design
- a carefully typeset biography
- warm, high-end print design

The interface should feel held, not swiped.

---

## Tone And Atmosphere

Imagine:

- warm paper
- soft daylight through linen curtains
- matte photographs mounted on thick paper
- serif headlines with real gravity
- hairline dividers
- gentle shadows
- weathered quietness
- stillness instead of velocity

There should be emotional seriousness without becoming funereal or gothic.

This is not a mourning app. It is a life archive. The design must hold:

- joy
- grief
- tenderness
- age
- absence
- continuity
- intergenerational warmth

## Design Language

### Typography

Typography should feel literary, not startup-native.

- Display type: expressive serif with character
- Body type: readable serif for stories and transcriptions
- UI type: restrained sans-serif for controls only

The hierarchy should imply:

- content matters more than controls
- names matter more than labels
- stories matter more than interface chrome

Typography should suggest:

- book design
- biography
- archival captions
- museum labeling

Avoid:

- all-caps-heavy UI
- geometric startup branding
- oversized, loud sans headings
- overly sentimental script fonts
- futuristic type

### Color

The palette should be warm, muted, and literary.

Core palette direction:

- warm off-whites
- parchment creams
- soft oat and linen tones
- warm blacks and brown-charcoals
- muted moss green
- terracotta-rose accents
- occasional gilt/brass note

The app should never drift into:

- pure white and blue SaaS
- purple gradients
- cyberpunk
- black glass luxury
- bright social-app color pops

### Materials

Preferred material feeling:

- paper
- cloth
- matte ink
- brass
- wood
- linen
- faded photographic paper

The UI should feel tactile even when fully digital.

### Motion

Motion should feel like breathing, not reacting.

- slow cross-fades
- gentle zooms
- subtle drift
- page-like transitions
- calm hover states

Avoid:

- bouncy springs
- confetti
- overscaled hover effects
- aggressive parallax
- “content carousel” behavior

## The Main Surfaces

These are the key product surfaces that inspirational imagery should help visualize.

## 1. The Atrium

The atrium is the emotional front door.

It is not a dashboard.

It should feel like:

- entering a softly lit family archive
- beginning from one memory
- sensing the scale and shape of the family without being overwhelmed

The atrium should include:

- one dominant featured memory
- contextual branch language
- a quiet sense of family scale
- a guided path into nearby stories
- a sense of family presence

The atrium should not be composed as:

- KPI cards
- admin widgets
- a stack of rails
- a filter-heavy content browser

Visually, the atrium should feel editorial and composed. One strong opening memory should anchor the experience.

## 2. Person Pages

A person page is not a profile. It is closer to a chapter in a living family book.

It should feel like:

- a person’s chapter in a biography
- a memory album with narrative structure
- a collected archive of voice, image, story, and relationships

Content may include:

- portrait
- essence line
- stories
- photos
- voice memos with transcripts
- timeline elements
- family connections
- places
- artifacts

These pages should read as long-form, immersive, and human.

## 3. Constellation / Tree View

The family tree should not look like genealogy software.

It should feel like:

- a constellation
- a field of people connected by delicate lines
- a memory cosmos

The tree is spatial and atmospheric, not rigid and bureaucratic.

Avoid:

- box-and-line org chart energy
- spreadsheet genealogy UI
- collapsible enterprise tree controls

## 4. Drift Mode

Drift mode is the app at its most poetic.

It should feel like:

- a film assembled from family memory
- one memory appearing, then another
- slow cross-fading through time
- a private projected archive

This surface can be especially useful for inspirational imagery because it embodies the app’s emotional promise.

---

## Key Product Values The UI Must Express

### 1. Memory Before Metrics

The app is about people and memory, not counts and system status.

Metrics may exist, but they are never the emotional center of a page.

### 2. Presence Before Utility

Before the interface asks the user to manage, sort, or edit, it should first let them feel where they are.

### 3. Branch Context Before Abstract Data

The family should feel lived-in and relational.

The app should communicate:

- who this memory belongs to
- where it sits in the family
- who else is nearby
- how the branch widens outward

### 4. Sparse Is Honest

Not every person will have a full archive.

The design must make sparse pages feel dignified, not empty or failed.

### 5. Permanence Is A Feature

The interface should feel trustworthy and durable.

It should suggest:

- careful keeping
- continuity
- preservation
- longevity

## What Makes This Different From Other Family Apps

Most family-oriented digital products drift toward one of these:

- genealogy tools
- photo apps
- messaging/social tools
- productivity/admin systems

Heirloom is intentionally none of those.

Its differentiation is not a feature checklist. Its differentiation is posture.

That posture is:

- memorial rather than social
- editorial rather than feed-based
- private rather than performative
- patient rather than addictive
- emotional rather than transactional

Any inspiration image that looks like a generalized “family social media app” is missing the point.

---

## What To Emphasize In Inspirational Images

When generating images, favor compositions that communicate:

- warm paper-like surfaces
- rich serif typography
- mounted photographs
- quiet, spacious layout
- deep but muted contrast
- elegant headings
- family connectedness without chart rigidity
- literary and archival atmosphere
- emotional stillness
- memory as physical presence

Good image directions:

- “a family archive app as a museum monograph”
- “a digital memorial library for a family”
- “a private memory atlas”
- “an editorial interface for intergenerational stories”
- “a still, warm, archival family record”

Avoid images that emphasize:

- dashboards
- business analytics
- social feeds
- bright badges
- generic app cards
- chatbot-heavy interfaces
- sterile minimalism
- overly rustic scrapbook craft aesthetics

---

## The Atrium In Particular

The atrium is the most important surface to visualize.

The specific target is:

**a memory-first editorial landing page for a single family tree**

The atrium should begin from:

- one featured memory
- one branch cue
- one pathway deeper into that branch

Then widen gently into:

- nearby stories
- historical span
- family presence

The atrium should feel like:

- a composed opening spread
- the first room in a family archive
- a scene, not a dashboard

It should not feel like:

- a homepage with modules
- a control panel
- a filter bar with content below it
- a content shelf system

If the inspirational image gets only one thing right, it should get this right.

---

## Visual Constraints For Generated Concepts

Generated concepts do not need to match the implementation literally, but they should obey these constraints:

- no smartphones filled with generic app grids
- no purple gradients
- no glassmorphism
- no high-saturation startup palettes
- no “AI product” visual clichés
- no obvious social media motifs
- no enterprise table layouts
- no large metric dashboards
- no rigid pedigree charts as the dominant visual
- no scrapbook clutter

Preferred composition traits:

- generous whitespace
- strong typographic hierarchy
- one dominant emotional focal point
- warm ambient light
- subtle texture
- layered paper-like surfaces
- understated controls
- human faces and names treated with dignity

## How To Judge Whether An Image Is On-Target

An image is on-target if it makes you think:

- "this feels like a family archive"
- "this feels emotionally safe"
- "this feels literary and real"
- "this feels like memory is being handled carefully"
- "this feels slower and wiser than a normal app"

An image is off-target if it makes you think:

- "this looks like a startup"
- "this looks like a family social network"
- "this looks like a genealogy tool"
- "this looks like a dashboard"
- "this looks generic"

## Short Creative Summary

Heirloom should look like a cross between:

- a monograph
- a memorial archive
- a museum reading room
- a family album
- and a constellation map

All translated into a quiet modern web application built for preserving memory with dignity.
